Abstract 
Public concern over safety and ethics hinders Tesla's autonomous vehicle fast adoption. This essay proposes a multi-
year strategy to regain trust. Collaboration, transparency, and public education build the foundation. Data-driven, iterative 
improvement guided by the Kaizen philosophy ensures continuous progress. By actively monitoring public perception and 
engaging with stakeholders, Tesla can position itself as a leader in responsible self-driving car development. 
1. 
Introduction 
Self-driving cars, also known as autonomous vehicles (AVs), represent a transformative development in transportation. 
Equipped with an array of sensors, sophisticated software, and powerful computers, these vehicles have the potential to 
navigate and operate entirely without human input. Proponents, like Fagnant et al. (2016), envision a future where AVs 
revolutionize our roads and cities, offering a multitude of potential benefits. 
Reduced accidents stand as a compelling advantage. Human error is a significant factor in a large portion of traffic 
accidents. Self-driving cars, with their precise sensor data and programmed responses, could drastically decrease the number 
of collisions. Studies like that by Talebian and Mahmassani (2018) suggest smoother traffic flow and reduced congestion as 
another benefit. Autonomous vehicles can communicate with each other, optimizing their movements and creating a more 
efficient transportation network. Perhaps the most impactful benefit lies in increased mobility for individuals who cannot drive 
themselves, such as the elderly, visually impaired, or those with disabilities (Shalev et al., 2017). 
However, the path towards widespread adoption of self-driving cars is fraught with challenges. Public concern 
regarding the safety and ethics of this technology has risen sharply in the wake of recent accidents involving Tesla vehicles 
operating on Autopilot. A particularly tragic example occurred on April 19th, 2024, in Snohomish County, Washington. 
According to news reports (Gillespie, 2023), a Tesla Model 3 on Autopilot struck a motorcycle while traveling on I-5 
northbound, tragically killing the 28-year-old motorcyclist. The incident also revealed that the Tesla driver was using their 
phone at the time, highlighting the critical need for robust safety measures and ethical considerations in the development and 
deployment of self-driving cars. 
This essay aims to propose a non-market strategy for Tesla to address these concerns. The goal is to regain public trust 
by tackling safety challenges, addressing ethical dilemmas head-on, and demonstrating a commitment to the responsible 
development of self-driving technology. 
2. 
Background and Literature review 
2.1 Safety Concerns Linger with Tesla's Self-Driving Technology 
Hofstede's framework of cultural dimensions identifies Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI) as a significant factor 
influencing societal attitudes towards new technologies. Societies with high UAI, characterized by a preference for clear rules 
and a discomfort with ambiguity, might be particularly apprehensive about the safety and ethical considerations surrounding 
self-driving cars. While Tesla's self-driving technology boasts advancements, significant safety challenges remain. Sensor 
limitations pose a major concern. Tesla's Autopilot and FSD primarily rely on cameras, which struggle with depth perception 
and varying lighting conditions compared to LiDAR technology. LiDAR uses lasers to create detailed 3D maps, offering 
advantages in low-light scenarios or with complex obstacles (NHTSA, 2023).  
This is a critical consideration for ensuring safe navigation in diverse environments. Unpredictable road conditions 
further complicate matters. Even the most advanced systems struggle with heavy rain, fog, or snow, as these factors can 
significantly impact sensor performance (NHTSA, 2023). Similarly, unexpected obstacles like debris or sudden manoeuvres 
by other drivers can challenge the algorithms' ability to react appropriately (Shalev et al., 2017). Data from Forbes Advisor 
support that safety concerns is the most important among respondents. 

2.2 Ethical Considerations: Programming Moral Dilemmas 
Beyond safety, ethical considerations become paramount when developing self-driving cars. Tesla's programming 
raises several key areas of concern. One major challenge involves equipping these vehicles to make ethical decisions in 
unavoidable accident scenarios, often referred to as the trolley problem. Programmers grapple with defining how the car should 
act when faced with a situation where harm is inevitable (Bonnefon et al., 2016). However, real-world complexities extend 
beyond these hypothetical dilemmas. Tesla's systems need to make split-second decisions in dynamic situations, considering 
factors like pedestrian safety, potential damage, and traffic flow. These choices raise intricate ethical questions that require 
careful programming and consideration. 
Researchers at the MIT Media Lab conducted a fascinating project called the Moral Machine Experiment 
(http://moralmachine.mit.edu/). This online platform presented participants with various trolley problem scenarios involving 
self-driving cars. The scenarios forced participants to choose between causing harm to a certain number of people or allowing 
the car to take an action with an uncertain outcome. The experiment's findings revealed significant variations in people's moral 
judgments, highlighting the complexity of programming self-driving cars to make ethically sound decisions in real-world 
situations. 
Acknowledging the complexities of ethical decision-making in self-driving cars, Tesla can adopt a three-pronged 
approach. First, transparency about limitations is crucial. Openly communicating the boundaries of the technology and the 
ethical considerations involved in its programming builds public trust and manages expectations. Second, prioritizing safety 
must be paramount. All programming decisions should be guided by ensuring the safety of drivers, pedestrians, and others on 
the road. Finally, Tesla can achieve evolving programming by continuously refining its algorithms based on real-world data 
and ongoing ethical discussions. This iterative approach allows Tesla to learn from experience and adapt its technology to 
address the complexities of real-world situations. 
2.3 Navigating the Evolving Regulatory Landscape: A Political Economy Perspective 
The regulatory landscape for self-driving cars in the United States is a complex interplay of politics and international 
business considerations. Currently, the absence of established federal regulations creates a fragmented system with varying 
state-level requirements. 
Through the lens of political economy, we can see how different stakeholders influence the regulatory landscape. 
Companies like Tesla have lobbied for lighter regulations, as evidenced by their past advocacy efforts documented in news 
articles like "Tesla Pushes for Relaxed Rules on Self-Driving Cars" (LAist, 2023). Conversely, labor unions, such as the United 
Auto Workers (UAW), have expressed concerns about job displacement due to automation and might advocate for regulations 
that prioritize human jobs in the industry (Reuters, 2023). Additionally, safety advocacy groups like Consumers Union 
consistently push for stricter safety standards and rigorous testing procedures before widespread deployment of self-driving 
cars (Consumer Reports, 2023). This interplay of interests creates a complex dynamic that policymakers must navigate. 
Beyond domestic concerns, the US needs to consider how its regulations compare to those in competitor nations. 
Countries like China, with its "China Standards 2025" initiative, are heavily invested in developing self-driving technology 
and have implemented their own regulatory frameworks (BBC, 2023). These frameworks could potentially influence the global 
market and pressure the US to adopt similar regulations to remain competitive.  Furthermore, international trade agreements 
like the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) could limit the US government's ability to regulate self-driving cars 
in ways that could disadvantage domestic companies compared to their counterparts in Canada and Mexico. 
3. 
Theoretical Framework 
Regaining societal acceptance for its self-driving technology presents a complex non-market challenge for Tesla. To 
analyse this challenge effectively, Bach and Allen's (2010) (IA)3 framework provide a valuable structure. This framework 
focuses on six key elements: Issues, Actors, Interests, Arenas, Information, and Assets. By applying these elements to Tesla's 
situation, we can gain a deeper understanding of the public's anxieties (Issues) surrounding safety and ethics of Autopilot. The 
framework also helps identify the key players involved (Actors) such as Tesla itself, regulators (NHTSA), research institutions, 
and the public, each with their own motivations (Interests). Media reports, industry conferences, and government hearings 
(Arenas) serve as platforms where these concerns are debated, and information is shared. Finally, Tesla's technological 
expertise, brand recognition, and vast amount of data collected (Assets) can be leveraged to develop solutions and rebuild 
societal acceptance.

Issues (I): In this context, the "Issues" refer to the specific challenges Tesla faces in regaining public trust for its self-
driving technology. These primarily stem from concerns about the safety of Autopilot, fuelled by accidents involving Tesla 
vehicles operating on the system. Additionally, anxieties exist regarding the ethical implications of programming decisions for 
unavoidable accidents and potential biases within the AI algorithms. Further complicating this issue is the cultural context. 
Geert Hofstede's framework highlights how Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI) varies significantly across countries. The United 
States, for instance, scores relatively low (46) on the UAI dimension compared to the world average (64). A low UAI ranking 
indicates a society that is more comfortable with ambiguity and less reliant on strict rules. This cultural characteristic can 
create a unique challenge for Tesla, as the public grapples with the inherent uncertainty associated with self-driving technology. 
However, in countries with a high UAI score, public anxieties surrounding self-driving cars might be even more pronounced 
due to a greater societal preference for clear regulations and a discomfort with ambiguity. 
Actors (A): The safe development and deployment of self-driving cars requires collaboration between several key 
stakeholders. Tesla, as the developer of Autopilot, has a critical responsibility to address safety concerns and rebuild public 
trust. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) plays a vital role by investigating accidents and 
potentially establishing regulations based on the safety data they gather. Beyond these two entities, research institutions and 
industry groups provide essential expertise.  For instance, the Ethics and Governance of Artificial Intelligence Initiative 
(EGAI) tackles the ethical considerations of self-driving cars, focusing on how these vehicles should make decisions.  
Meanwhile, the Self-Driving Car Coalition, comprised of industry leaders, advocates for responsible deployment, and conducts 
research on safety best practices. Ultimately, the public is the driving force behind the demand for safe transportation options.  
Consumers rely on clear information about Autopilot's capabilities and limitations. Organizations like Consumer Reports 
provide independent testing and safety evaluations, empowering consumers to make informed decisions. Additionally, AAA 
gauges public concerns and educates consumers on using existing driver-assistance features, ensuring a smooth transition 
towards potentially autonomous vehicles in the future. 
Interests (I): Each actor involved has its own motivations. Tesla naturally wants to maintain its market position in 
self-driving technology and avoid stricter regulations. NHTSA prioritizes public safety and ensuring responsible development 
of self-driving cars. Research institutions and industry groups seek to contribute to the development of safe and ethical self-
driving technology. Ultimately, the public wanted safe roads and reliable information to make informed decisions about this 
technology. 
Arenas (A): The public perception of Autopilot safety is shaped through various platforms. Media reports play a 
significant role, with news outlets covering accidents and safety concerns, potentially amplifying public anxieties. Government 
hearings provide forums where Tesla, NHTSA, and the public can discuss safety data, regulations, and potential improvements 
to Autopilot. Social media emerges as a powerful arena where the public shares opinions and concerns about Autopilot 
accidents, influencing overall perception. Industry conferences serve as another platform for experts to discuss advancements 
and limitations of self-driving car technology. 
Information (I): Data and evidence are crucial for addressing safety concerns and ethical dilemmas. Here, information 
sources include accident data involving Tesla vehicles operating on Autopilot, sourced from NHTSA investigations or public 
records. Public opinion polls gauging public perception of Autopilot safety provide valuable insights. Additionally, research 
on factors contributing to accidents involving Autopilot systems sheds light on potential areas for improvement. Data on the 
performance of camera and LiDAR systems in various driving conditions becomes relevant when considering sensor 

limitations. Finally, research on ethical frameworks for self-driving car programming informs strategies to address concerns 
about AI decision-making. 
Assets (A): Fortunately, Tesla possesses several valuable assets that can be leveraged to enhance Autopilot safety and 
rebuild trust. Tesla's technological expertise in self-driving car development can be channelled into improving Autopilot's 
safety features, such as developing more robust accident-avoidance systems. The vast amount of data collected from Tesla 
vehicles offers another asset. This data can be used to identify areas for improvement in Autopilot and understand the root 
causes of accidents. Finally, Tesla's brand recognition, its reputation for innovation, can be effectively used to communicate 
safety improvements and ethical considerations to the public. 
The (IA)3 framework by Bach and Allen (2010) offers a valuable lens for analysing the complexities surrounding 
Tesla's societal acceptance challenge with its self-driving technology. By dissecting the Issues, Actors, Interests, Arenas, 
Information, and Assets, we gain a nuanced understanding of the anxieties, motivations, and resources at play.

Regaining Public confidence: A Non-Market Strategy for Tesla's Self-Driving Technology 
Tesla faces a significant challenge in regaining public confidence for its self-driving technology, Autopilot. Public 
anxieties revolve around both safety concerns, fuelled by accidents involving Tesla vehicles operating on Autopilot, and ethical 
dilemmas related to programming decisions for unavoidable accidents and potential biases within the AI algorithms. To 
navigate this complex landscape, Tesla can implement a comprehensive non-market strategy built on collaboration, 
transparency, public education, advocacy, data utilization, and ongoing evaluation. 
4.1 Building Trust Through Partnerships Advocacy and Transparency (0-1 year) 
The first step in rebuilding trust is fostering collaboration with key actors. Partnering with leading research institutions 
allows Tesla to leverage their expertise for rigorous safety testing under diverse real-world conditions. These collaborations 
can extend to ethical considerations, jointly tackling the "trolley problem" and potential algorithm biases. Additionally, Tesla 
can work with industry groups like the SAE International to advocate for clear and comprehensive government regulations 
that address safety standards, ethical considerations, and data privacy. 
Open communication with the public is equally crucial. Tesla can increase transparency by clearly communicating 
Autopilot's limitations, emphasizing it as a driver-assistance system rather than a fully autonomous technology. Proactive 
addressing of public concerns raised in media reports and online forums demonstrates attentiveness and fosters trust. Regular 
safety reports detailing accident data, corrective actions taken, and ongoing improvements further demonstrate a commitment 
to responsible development. 
The initial focus is on establishing collaborations with research institutions and industry groups for safety testing and 
ethical considerations. This involves upfront costs for partnerships (potentially millions annually) and developing public 
education materials (tens of thousands to millions depending on scope). While initial impact might be limited, this section lays 
the groundwork for transparency by clearly communicating Autopilot limitations and addressing public concerns. 
4.2 Public Education and Advocacy for Responsible Development (0-3 years) 
Public education campaigns are crucial for shaping public perception of self-driving technology. Tesla can target these 
campaigns strategically towards different audiences. Educating drivers on the proper use of Autopilot features is essential, 
with campaigns emphasizing the importance of attentiveness and supervision.  Policymakers require clear and comprehensive 
regulations, and Tesla's public education efforts can highlight the importance of prioritizing public safety, ethical 
considerations, and data privacy in regulations governing the development and deployment of self-driving cars.  For the public, 
raising awareness about the capabilities and limitations of this technology is key to responsible public discourse. Educational 
campaigns can showcase the potential benefits, such as increased accessibility for disabled individuals or reduced traffic 
congestion.  
Beyond education, active advocacy is vital. Tesla can work with industry groups like a prominent organization 
representing state-level highway safety offices, such as the Governors Highway Safety Association (GHSA). This 
collaboration allows Tesla to directly engage with policymakers who play a significant role in shaping state-specific 
regulations for self-driving cars. By engaging with policymakers and advocating for regulations that prioritize safety and 
ethical considerations, Tesla demonstrates its leadership in the responsible development of self-driving technology. Working 
collaboratively with industry groups and policymakers allows Tesla to build public trust, paving the way for a safer future with 
self-driving cars. 
Public education campaigns (tens of thousands to millions) and advocacy efforts aim to inform drivers on proper 
Autopilot usage, raise public awareness about the technology's capabilities and limitations, and influence regulations that 
prioritize safety, ethics, and data privacy.  While swaying public anxieties completely might be difficult, this may work towards 
responsible development.

4.3 Leveraging Data for Improvement and Continuous Monitoring (1+ years) 
Tesla's vast reservoir of data from its vehicles presents a powerful opportunity for continuous improvement, perfectly 
aligning with the Kaizen philosophy (Womack & Jones, 2003). Kaizen, a Japanese concept, emphasizes making small, iterative 
improvements over time. This approach is ideal for the complex and ever-evolving world of self-driving technology. Tesla 
can leverage Kaizen through data analysis in several ways. Firstly, by analysing data from near-misses and accidents, Tesla 
can pinpoint weaknesses in its self-driving algorithms and sensor performance. This continuous process of identifying and 
addressing issues aligns with the core Kaizen tenet of eliminating waste, in this case, safety risks. 
Another crucial aspect is mitigating potential biases within Tesla's AI algorithms. Data analysis helps detect patterns 
that might indicate unfair decision-making. This focus on fairness aligns with the Kaizen principle of respecting people, 
ensuring the technology is inclusive and avoids discriminatory outcomes. The benefits of data extend beyond safety and bias 
mitigation. analysing driving behaviours and performance data allows Tesla to identify areas where the self-driving technology 
can be further refined for smoother and more efficient operation. This continuous optimization aligns with the Kaizen principle 
of striving for better performance in every aspect. While implementing this Kaizen-like approach requires significant 
investments (millions annually) in data infrastructure, personnel, and potential upgrades to vehicles, the potential benefits are 
substantial. Data analysis can lead to improved Autopilot safety, mitigate algorithmic biases, and identify areas for enhanced 
performance and efficiency. 
4.4 Monitoring and Evaluation: A Crucial Loop (3+ years) 
Building trust in a new technology like self-driving cars requires a commitment to continuous monitoring and 
collaboration, similar to Airbus' prioritization of public trust in air travel safety after the Boeing 737 MAX crashes (Clemens, 
2019). This doesn't require bad-mouthing competitors, but rather demonstrably prioritizing safety and responsible 
development. Tesla can achieve this through a multi-pronged approach.  First, by regularly monitoring public opinion polls 
and social media sentiment analysis, Tesla can gauge public confidence in its self-driving technology. Identifying shifts in 
perception allows for timely adaptation of their approach. Addressing concerns promptly fosters trust and demonstrates 
responsiveness. 
Secondly, continuous tracking of accident data, involving both Autopilot-engaged and non-engaged vehicles, is 
crucial. analysing this data helps identify trends and areas for safety improvement. Additionally, monitoring media reports 
allows Tesla to stay abreast of emerging concerns and adjust communication strategies accordingly. Proactive media 
engagement can be used to share safety monitoring results and highlight collaborations with industry groups on safety 
standards. Finally, actively collaborating with industry groups on safety standards and best practices demonstrates Tesla's 
commitment to a safe future for all self-driving car users, regardless of brand. This collaborative spirit, coupled with the 
continuous feedback loop from monitoring efforts, fosters public trust and positions Tesla as a leader in responsible self-
driving car development. 
5. 
Conclusion 
Regaining public trust in Tesla's self-driving technology requires a strategic multi-year approach. The initial year 
focuses on building a strong foundation through collaboration and transparency. Partnering with research institutions ensures 
rigorous safety testing under diverse real-world conditions. Collaboration with industry groups tackles ethical considerations 
and advocates for clear regulations prioritizing safety, data privacy, and ethical development. Open communication is key, 
with Tesla clearly outlining Autopilot's limitations and proactively addressing public concerns raised in media and online 
forums.  
Years two to three involve public education and advocacy. Targeted campaigns educate drivers on proper Autopilot 
use and policymakers on the importance of regulations that prioritize safety, ethics, and data privacy. Public awareness 
campaigns highlight the technology's potential benefits, like increased accessibility and reduced congestion. Active advocacy 
involves working with industry groups like the Governors Highway Safety Association to directly engage policymakers and 
influence regulations for safe and ethical self-driving car development. 
Beyond the initial years, Tesla can leverage its vast data for continuous improvement. Inspired by the Kaizen 
philosophy of iterative improvement, data analysis from near-misses and accidents helps identify weaknesses in algorithms 
and sensor performance. This data is also crucial for mitigating potential biases within AI algorithms, ensuring fair and 
inclusive decision-making. Additionally, analysing driving behaviours and performance data allows for continuous refinement, 
leading to smoother and more efficient operation. 
Finally, a commitment to monitoring and evaluation is crucial for long-term success. Regularly monitoring public 
opinion polls and social media sentiment analysis allows Tesla to adapt its strategies based on real-time public perception. 
Continuous tracking of accident data, both Autopilot-engaged and non-engaged vehicles, helps identify trends and areas for 
safety improvement. Monitoring media reports allows for proactive engagement with emerging concerns. Most importantly, 

actively collaborating with industry groups on safety standards and best practices demonstrates Tesla's dedication to a safe 
future for all self-driving car users, regardless of brand. By implementing this comprehensive strategy, Tesla can rebuild public 
trust, position itself as a leader in responsible self-driving car development, and pave the way for a future where autonomous 
vehicles safely transform our roads and cities.
